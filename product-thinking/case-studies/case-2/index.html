Case Study
Google & Project Nimbus

The Senario

Google made a ~$1.2 billion cloud-computing deal with the Israeli government in 2021, aiming to provide them with an "all-encompassing cloud solution". However, this took place during the Israeli-Palestinian conflict, sparking controversey in the media and Google itself. Google had 50 employees protest the deal due to human rigts concerns, with some disrupting the company's day-to-day operations and efficiency. Google responded by firing all 50 employees immediately.

The Problem

What decisions could have been made differently at Google to maintain both their reputation and revenue?

Options Considered

Stay in the deal (best financially)
Renegotiate the deal (better for revenue and media-response) Back out of the deal entirely (lose out on the revenue, but help reputation)

Decision & Rationale

While Google had every right to fire all 50 employees, their immediate termination made Google seem harsh and uncaring for their employees, especially given their reputation as an "open" company that welcomes criticism from its employees. Since they already fired their employees, renegotiating the deal to ensure no cloud-computing would be used for anything relating to the violation of human rights. It would allow Google to save face while still getting the revenue from the deal. Both staying in the deal and backing out of it are extreme cases that hurt Google in opposite ways; staying in ruins its reuputation of being an honest, open company, while backing out makes other large deals harder to make as Google will now have a reputation for backing out of nearly-completed deals.

Expected Impact

Short-term, Google will face criticism for their termination of 50 employees who protested them. Long-term, Google will be known as a company that values revenue over employees, staining their reputation with the controversey.

Reflection

As this case was more about ethics, it was interetsing to see how business deals can have huge impacts on a company's employees as well as its media-response.
